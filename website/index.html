SAED Digital Twin Findings

Economic dispatch ensures electricity demand is served optimally across grid components. By simulating this using a digital twin calibrated with ten years of historical PGCB grid data (2015–2025), we analyzed operational patterns, cost boundaries, and limits governing Bangladesh's transmission network.

1. Operational Savings on Expensive Fuel Peak Units

The grid historically relied heavily on liquid fuel peaking generators costing $180/MWh. Under the SAED optimal merit-order dispatch, liquid fuel generation is mostly bypassed in 2025, substituting cheaper gas ($45/MWh) and coal ($65/MWh). This reveals significant recoverable grid efficiencies.

Liquid Fuel cost ($180/MWh) vs Gas ($45/MWh)

2. Governing Power with Seasonality Coefficients (α Matrix)

Availability metrics dictate the seasonal fuel mix. Hydroelectric dispatch ranges from 41% availability in winter (due to low water levels) up to 100% capacity in the monsoon season. Solar peaks in the pre-monsoon (100%) but scales back to 69% in the monsoon due to cloud cover.

Hydro limits: α = 0.41 (Winter) → 1.00 (Monsoon)

3. Capacity Constraints Emerge by 2026 Without New Additions

With demand anchored to the actual 2024–2025 operational level (~13 GW Monsoon average) and growing at ~7.9% annually, stress test projections show that reserve margins breach the 8% planning floor during Monsoon and Pre-monsoon peak hours as early as 2026–2027 — assuming no new capacity is commissioned. This underscores the urgency of pipeline plant additions in Bangladesh's power development plan.

Reserve breach (no new capacity scenario): Year ~2026–2027

Calibration Metrics

The digital twin is anchored to the actual 2024–2025 grid operational level. Validation against the historical generation mix yields MAE values of 351–650 MW. The divergence reflects deliberate differences: the twin dispatches sources in pure merit order, while the real grid is constrained by fuel-supply contracts, transmission congestion, and scheduling inertia.

Season MAE (MW) Interpretation
Winter 351 MW Stable base load operation, minimal peaking required.
Pre-monsoon 650 MW Transition period, solar ramp and high AC load variability.
Monsoon 560 MW Highest load uncertainty, peaking dispatch active.
Post-monsoon 363 MW Post-peak cool down, lowest dispatch error.
